Boston Through Parking

A black-and-white visual story of urban activity using Boston monthly parking transactions by zone.

What this dataset captures

This dataset records monthly parking transactions for 572 Boston zones. Each row corresponds to a street segment or zone, and each value reflects how many parking transactions occurred there during a given month. In practice, these counts can act as a proxy for urban movement. Higher transaction counts suggest stronger activity, whether from work commutes, shopping, or other city use. The analysis reveals two important realities at once: Boston becomes dramatically busier from winter into summer, and the dataset itself contains missing months later in the year. Together, those features make it a strong case study for storytelling with real-world data.

1. Activity over time

The monthly totals show a strong upward trajectory from January through June. Activity begins at just 2,135 transactions in January, rises steadily through spring, and peaks at 78,789 in June. July remains high at 35,501 but drops sharply after the June peak. August and the remaining later months appear as zero in this dataset, which suggests missing data rather than a true collapse in urban activity. This chart establishes the central rhythm of the story: city movement grows as the weather improves, and the dataset records that shift very clearly during the first seven months of the year.

2. Month-to-month change

Looking at change rather than raw totals makes the acceleration even clearer. February grows by 2,587 compared with January, March adds another 5,017, and April jumps by 22,633. The rise continues into May and then surges again into June, which adds 31,998 transactions over May. This makes June the key turning point in the dataset. The negative changes afterward are also informative, although they must be interpreted carefully because August onward is missing. This view is useful because it shows not only that Boston gets busier over time, but also exactly when the largest shifts in city activity occur.

3. Seasonal comparison

Seasonal averages make the contrast especially strong. Winter averages about 5.99 transactions per zone per month, spring rises to about 51.81, and summer reaches about 66.60. Even with missing data later in summer, the pattern is still unmistakable: Boston is far more active in warmer months. This is one of the clearest insights in the project because it connects the parking data to human behavior. Seasonal weather, tourism, and daily mobility likely all contribute to this rise. The city does not simply become slightly busier in summer. It becomes dramatically more active compared with the winter baseline.

4. Where activity concentrates

Parking activity is not evenly distributed across Boston. The ten busiest zones alone account for 14.35% of all transactions in the dataset, which is a large share given there are 572 zones in total. This concentration suggests that a relatively small set of streets functions as recurring urban hotspots. Many of the highest-ranked segments are clustered around Newbury and Boylston streets, reinforcing the idea that commercial and highly visited areas dominate usage. This chart does not just identify the busiest locations. It shows that Boston’s parking landscape is structured around a few especially active corridors rather than being spread uniformly across the city.

5. The quietest zones

The bottom of the distribution is just as revealing as the top. Several zones record zero total transactions, while a handful of others record only one transaction across the whole dataset. This stark contrast shows how uneven urban usage can be. Some street segments operate as daily magnets for movement, while others barely register at all. Those low values may reflect residential character, lower demand, incomplete coverage, or operational differences in how parking was recorded. Including the least active zones strengthens the story because it makes the concentration at the top more meaningful. Boston’s parking activity is not simply high or low. It is highly uneven.

6. How the top zones behave over time

The five busiest zones do not all follow exactly the same monthly pattern, but they all rise strongly into spring and early summer. North Charles between Boylston and Beacon peaks at 841 in June, while South Newbury between Mass and Charlesgate East rises especially sharply, reaching 1,289 in June after almost no winter activity. This makes the chart useful for comparing consistency versus seasonality. Some zones are already active early in the year, while others explode later. Together, these trajectories suggest that even among Boston’s busiest streets, there are different types of urban behavior: stable corridors, sharply seasonal areas, and strong spring-to-summer climbers.

7. Who peaks when?

Most zones reach their highest recorded month in June. In fact, 421 of the 572 zones peak there. That number is much larger than any other month and reinforces the importance of June as the central high-activity moment in the dataset. July is next but far behind, while April and May capture smaller groups of zones. Only three zones peak in January. This distribution matters because it shows that the seasonal story is not driven by just a few outliers. A very large share of the city follows the same general pattern: movement builds steadily and reaches its strongest point in early summer.

8. Share of activity

A doughnut view makes the concentration of activity easy to read. The top ten zones contribute 14.35% of all recorded transactions, while the remaining 562 zones contribute 85.65%. At first glance that may not seem overwhelming, but given the number of total zones, it is substantial. This means a tiny fraction of zones carries a disproportionately large share of recorded parking behavior. The result supports the broader narrative that urban activity clusters spatially. Even within a city as complex as Boston, movement and parking demand are not distributed equally. They accumulate in a few strong and repeatedly used locations.

9. Spatial perspective

A map adds geographic context to the patterns shown above. The dataset itself does not include coordinates, so the points below are illustrative placements centered around the Back Bay and nearby Boston areas associated with the top-ranked street segments. Even so, the map is useful because it grounds the story in physical space. Rather than reading the zones as abstract names, viewers can place them in relation to the city. This strengthens the interpretation that much of the strongest activity is tied to dense and highly visited parts of Boston. Spatial framing helps transform a table of transactions into a narrative about urban geography.

Map markers are illustrative because the dataset does not include lat/long coordinates.

10. Data limitations matter

One of the most important findings in this project is not about traffic or hotspots. It is about data quality. August through December appear as zero in the monthly totals, and the notebook confirms that September, October, and November sum to zero. That pattern is unrealistic for a major city and strongly suggests missing or incomplete reporting rather than true inactivity. This is a valuable storytelling moment because real-world analysis is never just about making charts. It also requires evaluating what the dataset can and cannot support. In this case, the strongest conclusions come from January through July, while the later months must be interpreted with caution.
